# Option Feedback

Feedback should be specific, conversational, and complete. Treat it as a brief teaching moment, not a score report. Keep it concise, but never sacrifice the explanation just to minimize the sentence count.

These rules apply when every answer option has its own feedback.

## Correct-Option Feedback

Explain why the correct option works. Show how the decisive evidence connects to the lesson concept and leads to the answer or practical result instead of merely restating the option.

## Wrong-Option Feedback

Every wrong option's feedback must complete all three of these jobs:

1. Explain why that selected option is wrong for this exact question, using the evidence, rule, or lesson concept that rules it out.
2. Explicitly identify the correct option, or an unmistakable paraphrase of it.
3. Teach why the correct option follows by connecting the situation's evidence to the relevant concept, mechanism, or rule and then to a concrete result or consequence.

Do not stop after saying the selected option is irrelevant, incomplete, unsupported, or focused on the wrong part of the situation. A line such as "The correct answer is X" does not complete the correction unless it also explains what makes X correct. The learner should understand the full reasoning from the feedback itself, even if the interface also displays the correct answer.

These requirements describe the content, not a sentence template. Vary the structure and phrasing naturally across options. Lead with the misconception, decisive evidence, correct reasoning, or practical result according to what makes the explanation clearest.

## Plain Language

Write for a learner who may be encountering this distinction for the first time. Start with the concrete facts from the situation: name the relevant object, person, action, observation, or consequence. Then connect those facts to the domain concept or technical label when the label helps. Do not replace the explanation with compressed category shorthand or a chain of unexplained technical terms.

Avoid grade-report transitions such as "the correct answer is," "the correct effect is," or "the correct option shows." The interface already identifies the answer. State what actually happened and why that makes the answer correct.

When it clarifies the boundary between the options, explain what condition would have made the selected option correct and contrast it with what actually happened. Use this counterfactual as a teaching tool, not a fixed sentence pattern, and do not force it into every response.

## Relatable Teaching

Help the learner connect the correction to something concrete they can picture or use. Prefer the everyday consequence, familiar observation, practical decision, or real-world artifact already present in the situation. When the concept is still abstract, add a short familiar comparison that demonstrates the same mechanism.

Prefer explaining the existing situation in familiar terms over adding another analogy. Any comparison must be easier to understand than the original concept and demonstrate the same mechanism; omit it if it adds jargon or abstraction. Do not force an analogy into every option, and do not repeat one comparison mechanically across the lesson. For technical or professional topics, a familiar workflow, record, tool, or visible result can provide the relatable bridge.

## Grounding

Do not write generic feedback that would apply to any question. Do not introduce facts that are required to understand the correction but are absent from the question, its evidence, or the lesson source.
